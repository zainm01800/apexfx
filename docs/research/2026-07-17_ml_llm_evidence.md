# ML and LLMs for Trading: What Actually Works — Evidence Review

Prepared for a retail FX system using (a) rule-based momentum, (b) GBM meta-labeling (AFML-style), (c) LLM filters (DeepSeek/Groq news veto, LLM "lessons," LLM-mined rules). Codebase anchors: `engine/apex_quant/strategies/meta_labeling.py`, `engine/apex_quant/ml/`, `engine/apex_quant/ai/sentiment_filter.py`, `engine/apex_quant/risk/learning.py`.

## 0. Executive summary

- In equities, ML genuinely beats linear models on cross-sectional return prediction, but the gains are concentrated in small, expensive-to-trade stocks and have decayed sharply since ~2010 in the best-documented horse-race ([Krauss et al. 2017](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0377221716308657)). In FX majors, the benchmark to beat is the random walk, and 40 years of literature says almost nothing beats it reliably ([Rossi 2013](https://ideas.repec.org/a/aea/jeclit/v51y2013i4p1063-1119.html)). The strongest FX evidence is for simple trend/momentum — i.e., what component (a) already is.
- Meta-labeling has **no peer-reviewed validation as a live performance enhancer**. Practitioner reports describe it as a trade-count-reducing filter with mixed results. It is, however, the *least wrong* ML component if validated brutally (purged CV, embargo, deflated metrics).
- LLM news-sentiment has real academic support for *equities*, but the field's own flagship authors have shown LLM "forecasts" over training-data periods are untrustworthy due to memorization, and that the equity effect decays as adoption rises. For **FX there is essentially no published evidence** of durable LLM-sentiment edge. LLM "lessons" and LLM-mined rules have no credible supporting evidence at all and add invisible data-mining.
- The one "AI-adjacent" thing with strong evidence for a momentum system is **volatility-aware sizing / crash avoidance** — but as risk management, not as an alpha overlay.

## 1. ML for return prediction in liquid markets

**Equities: real but small, concentrated, and decaying.** Gu, Kelly & Xiu (2020, RFS) is the canonical positive result: trees and neural nets outperform linear models on monthly US stock returns, with much of the predictability from price/volume signals ([doi.org/10.1093/rfs/hhaa009](https://doi.org/10.1093/rfs/hhaa009)). But follow-ups qualify this heavily: ML predictability is concentrated in stocks with high limits to arbitrage (small, illiquid, costly to trade) ([Avramov, Cheng & Metzker 2023, Management Science](https://ideas.repec.org/a/inm/ormnsc/v69y2023i5p2587-2619.html)), and AQR's own assessment is that return prediction is "a small data science with low signal-to-noise ratios," with ML's more reliable uses being risk and trading-cost modeling, not alpha ([Israel, Kelly & Moskowitz 2020, SSRN 3624052](https://ssrn.com/abstract=3624052); quote via [Soehnholz ESG research book](https://soehnholzesg.com/de/wp-content/uploads/210806%20Das%20Soehnholz%20ESG%20und%20Impact%20Researchbuch.pdf)). The most direct GBM/RF horse-race — Krauss, Do & Huck (2017, EJOR) on S&P 500 constituents 1992–2015 — found ensemble daily returns of ~0.45% **before** transaction costs (DNN 0.33%, GBT 0.37%, RF 0.43%), evaluated at 0.05% cost per half-turn, and outperformance was a 1992–2009 phenomenon: "from 2010 the excess return fluctuates around zero" ([ScienceDirect](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0377221716308657); [EconStor full text](https://www.econstor.eu/bitstream/10419/130166/1/856307327.pdf); post-2010 decay quoted in [this survey](https://www.robertosaia.it/documents/publications/041_A_General_Framework.pdf); numbers confirmed in [Fischer & Krauss's follow-up working paper](https://www.statistik.rw.fau.de/files/2015/12/11-2017.pdf)). Note the counterpoint: Kelly, Malamud & Zhou (2024, JF) show complexity can be a virtue with very large data ([doi.org/10.1111/jofi.13298](https://doi.org/10.1111/jofi.13298)) — but that regime requires far more data than a single-account FX system generates.

**Decay after publication/costs is a first-order fact, not a footnote.** McLean & Pontiff (2016, JF): anomaly returns are ~26% lower out-of-sample and ~58% lower post-publication ([numbers as reported by Robeco](https://www.robeco.com/docm/docu-202003-how-to-navigate-the-factor-zoo-us.pdf) and [Jacobs & Müller](https://www.unicreditgroup.com/content/dam/unicreditgroup-eu/ucfoundation/WorkingPapers/2019/wp-135-Jacobs-e-Mueller.pdf)). [Chen & Zimmermann](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2209.13623) replicate ~50% post-publication decay and show it accrues smoothly over ~10 years. Hou, Xue & Zhang (2020, RFS) fail to replicate 65% of published anomalies ([NBER WP 23394](https://www.nber.org/papers/w23394)); Jensen, Kelly & Pedersen (2023, JF) are more optimistic using global data and Bayesian methods ([SSRN 3774514](https://papers.ssrn.com/sol3/papers.cfm?abstract_id=3774514)) — the honest reading is "a minority of signals are real and even those decay."

**FX majors: the bar is the random walk.** Since [Meese & Rogoff 1983](https://cepr.org/voxeu/columns/are-exchange-rates-predictable), exchange-rate models fail to beat a random walk out-of-sample at short horizons; Rossi's JEL survey concludes predictability is episodic — it depends on predictor, horizon, sample, and evaluation method ([Rossi 2013](https://ideas.repec.org/a/aea/jeclit/v51y2013i4p1063-1119.html)). What *does* have support in FX is trend/momentum, not ML: Hsu, Taylor & Wang (2016, JIE) find trend rules still profitable out-of-sample over 1975–2013 but with markedly weaker performance in recent years ([ScienceDirect](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0022199616300472)); Menkhoff, Sarno, Schmeling & Schrimpf (2012, JFE) document robust currency momentum but note transaction costs consume ~30–50% of returns and 3-year unprofitable stretches are normal ([JFE 106(3):660-684](https://ideas.repec.org/a/eee/jfinec/v106y2012i3p660-684.html); [authors' VoxEU summary](https://cepr.org/voxeu/columns/limits-currency-momentum-trading)). Time-series momentum/trend has the deepest cross-asset evidence base of any simple rule ([Moskowitz, Ooi & Pedersen 2012, JFE](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.jfineco.2011.11.003); [Hurst, Ooi & Pedersen 2017, JPM — positive every decade since 1880](https://doi.org/10.3905/jpm.2017.44.1.015); [full text](https://fairmodel.econ.yale.edu/ec439/hurst.pdf)).

**Direct answer to the GBM-vs-linear question:** in the best-documented comparison, GBMs/RFs on price features beat linear only pre-cost and pre-2010; after costs and in the modern sample the advantage converges to zero ([Krauss et al. 2017](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0377221716308657)). There is no credible published evidence that GBMs on technical features add net edge in *FX majors*. ML does work better where markets are less efficient — e.g., China's retail-dominated equity market ([Leippold, Wang & Zhou 2022, JFE](https://ideas.repec.org/a/eee/jfinec/v145y2022i2p64-82.html)) — which is the opposite of EURUSD.

## 2. Meta-labeling and triple-barrier labeling

The source material is a practitioner's book (Lopez de Prado, *Advances in Financial Machine Learning*, Wiley 2018) plus vendor documentation ([Hudson & Thames toy example](https://hudsonthames.org/meta-labeling-a-toy-example/), [mlfinlab summary](https://www.mlfinlab.com/en/latest/backtest_overfitting/seven_reasons.html)). **No peer-reviewed study demonstrates that meta-labeling improves a live strategy's net Sharpe.** Practitioner experience is lukewarm: a widely-cited QuantConnect thread is titled ["Why Meta-Labeling Is Not a Silver Bullet"](https://www.quantconnect.com/forum/discussion/14706/why-meta-labeling-is-not-a-silver-bullet/), and the top practitioner observation on [Quant StackExchange](https://quant.stackexchange.com/questions/50082/why-meta-labeling-is-is-robust) is that "it acts like a filter for the trades only" — i.e., its main measurable effect is reducing trade count, which mechanically raises precision while cutting expectancy per unit time and *itself becoming a tuned parameter*.

Known pitfalls, all documented in the source method and the overfitting literature:
- **Overlapping outcomes:** triple-barrier labels on adjacent signals share most of their forward path, so effective sample size is far below trade count; AFML's own remedies (uniqueness-based sample weights, purged K-fold CV with embargo, combinatorially-purged CV) exist precisely because naive CV on these labels leaks ([AFML book](https://www.wiley.com/en-us/Advances+in+Financial+Machine+Learning-p-9781119482086); implementation notes in [mlfinlab](https://www.mlfinlab.com/en/latest/backtest_overfitting/seven_reasons.html)).
- **Label leakage:** any feature that correlates with the path inside the barrier window (e.g., realized vol over the holding horizon, same-bar high/low excursions) contaminates the label; with triple-barrier labels the purge/embargo must span the full horizon.
- **The veto threshold is a mined parameter:** Lopez de Prado's own JPM paper lists wrong labeling and backtest overfitting among the [10 reasons most ML funds fail](https://doi.org/10.3905/jpm.2018.44.6.120).

To the system's credit, `MetaLabeledStrategy` already implements the right scaffolding: triple-barrier labels, calibrated P(win), an explicit veto gate, CPCV purge/embargo, and pass-through degradation with too few labels (`engine/apex_quant/strategies/meta_labeling.py:1-31`). The open question the evidence raises is not implementation quality but whether the secondary model's OOS veto beats a trivial baseline (e.g., "trade only if the primary's own win rate in the current regime exceeds X") net of costs — the literature gives no reason to expect it does on a few hundred FX trades.

## 3. LLMs for trading

**Sentiment signal — the real (equity) evidence.** The foundation is not LLMs at all: [Tetlock 2007 (JF)](https://doi.org/10.1111/j.1540-6261.2007.01232.x) showed negative media words predict downward price pressure and [Tetlock, Saar-Tsechansky & Macskassy 2008 (JF)](https://doi.org/10.1111/j.1540-6261.2008.01359.x) showed linguistic content predicts earnings and returns. The LLM chapter: Lopez-Lira & Tang (2023, subsequently in the Journal of Finance) found ChatGPT/GPT-4 headline scores predict next-day stock moves, concentrated in small stocks and negative news ([arXiv:2304.07619](https://arxiv.org/abs/2304.07619)). Two self-limiting findings in that same work: the headline 650% cumulative return was **without transaction costs** ([v5 PDF](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2304.07619v5)), and "strategy returns decline as LLM adoption rises, consistent with improved price efficiency" ([latest abstract](https://arxiv.org/abs/2304.07619)).

**The critiques are severe and partly from the same authors.** Lopez-Lira, Tang & Zhu (2025) demonstrate LLMs have memorized pre-cutoff economic data to recall-level accuracy — instructions and masking fail — so *any* backtest over the training window is unidentified: results are consistent with both skill and memorization ([arXiv:2504.14765](https://arxiv.org/abs/2504.14765)). Sarkar & Vafa (2024) formalize this as [lookahead bias in pretrained language models (SSRN 4754678)](https://papers.ssrn.com/sol3/papers.cfm?abstract_id=4754678); Gao, Jiang & Yan (2025) build a statistical test and find in-sample LLM predictability "is largely driven by look-ahead bias" ([arXiv:2512.23847](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2512.23847)). Glasserman & Lin (2023, Columbia) add a subtler "distraction effect": the LLM's general knowledge of a named company contaminates its sentiment reading — and they recommend anonymizing headlines, which *outperformed* raw headlines in-sample ([arXiv:2309.17322](https://arxiv.org/abs/2309.17322)). On numerical prediction, zero-shot ChatGPT underperforms even linear regression ([Xie et al. 2023, "Wall Street Neophyte"](https://arxiv.org/abs/2304.05351)).

**Where LLMs do earn their keep:** text *classification* against a human-defined rubric — GPT models beat prior NLP methods at classifying FOMC stance ([Hansen & Kazinnik, "Can ChatGPT Decipher Fedspeak?" (Richmond Fed)](https://www.frbsf.org/research-and-insights/publications/system-research-richmond-fed/2024/03/can-chatgpt-decipher-fedspeak/)) — i.e., turning messy text into clean categorical features, not predicting returns.

**Failure modes specific to your three LLM uses:**
- *News veto:* no FX-specific published evidence exists; the equity evidence is pre-cost and decaying; and a DeepSeek/Groq model's pre-cutoff memorization makes retrospective evaluation of the veto essentially meaningless ([arXiv:2504.14765](https://arxiv.org/abs/2504.14765)). It can only be validated forward, on logged live decisions. The fail-ALLOW design in `sentiment_filter.py:15-16` is the right instinct.
- *Non-stationarity of the model itself:* the same API model changes behavior over time — GPT-4's prime/composite accuracy fell from 84% to 51% between two 2023 snapshots ([Chen, Zaharia & Zou 2023](https://arxiv.org/abs/2307.09009)). A "calibrated" veto threshold silently uncalibrates with every provider update.
- *LLM "lessons" fed into prompts:* no credible study shows durable edge. The failure mechanics are known: hallucination is a documented, persistent property of LLMs ([Ji et al. 2023, ACM Computing Surveys survey](https://arxiv.org/abs/2202.03629)); outputs drift ([2307.09009](https://arxiv.org/abs/2307.09009)); and injecting free-text "lessons" is an untestable feature channel — each lesson is effectively a new trading rule added without any of the validation in §5, and it compounds invisibly.
- *LLM-mined rules:* LLMs make beautiful backtests trivially cheap to produce, which means more candidate strategies, more multiple testing, more false discoveries — practitioners who both build ML systems and trade them describe this as "more of the disease, faster" ([Robot Wealth essays](https://robotwealth.com/author/robot-master/), practitioner source — treat as opinion, but it correctly applies the multiple-testing math of §5). Mining is defensible only as *hypothesis generation* gated by the full validation protocol.

## 4. Volatility-regime filters for momentum

This is the strongest evidence-backed overlay for a momentum system, with important caveats:
- Momentum's risk is time-varying and partly predictable: Barroso & Santa-Clara (2015, JFE) show scaling momentum exposure by its own recent realized vol nearly doubled its Sharpe and virtually eliminated crashes in a long US sample ([doi.org/10.1016/j.jfineco.2014.11.010](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.jfineco.2014.11.010); [summary](https://alphaarchitect.com/cross-sectional-momentum/)). Daniel & Moskowitz (2016, JFE) document that momentum crashes (1932, 2009) cluster in post-panic, high-vol rebound states and design dynamic strategies that mitigate them ([doi.org/10.1016/j.jfineco.2015.12.002](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.jfineco.2015.12.002)).
- **But** the aggressive "scale by inverse variance" version does not survive scrutiny: across 103 equity strategies, real-time vol-managed portfolios generally earn *lower* certainty-equivalent returns and Sharpe than unmanaged ones, with significant improvement in only 8/103 cases (all momentum-related) ([Cederburg, O'Doherty, Wang & Yan 2020, JFE](https://ideas.repec.org/a/eee/jfinec/v138y2020i1p95-117.html)); after transaction costs, vol management of factors other than the market produces zero abnormal returns and *reduces* Sharpe ([Barroso & Detzel 2021, JFE](https://ideas.repec.org/a/eee/jfinec/v140y2021i3p744-767.html)); long-term investors do not benefit from vol timing ([Moreira & Muir 2019, JFE](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.jfineco.2018.09.010)); and the apparent gains of vol scaling in time-series momentum are largely just dynamic leverage ([Kim, Tse & Wald 2016, Journal of Financial Markets 30:103-124](https://arxiv.org/html/2310.10500v2) — reference listing with citation details).
- Harvey, Hoyle et al. (2018, JPM, Man Group) give the practical synthesis: vol targeting's reliable benefit is **drawdown and left-tail reduction**, not raw return enhancement ([doi.org/10.3905/jpm.2018.45.1.014](https://doi.org/10.3905/jpm.2018.45.1.014)).

Reading: a fixed vol target for position sizing, plus a "reduce/stand down when the strategy's own recent vol spikes" crash-avoidance rule, is evidence-backed risk hygiene for momentum. A continuously re-optimized regime-timing overlay is not.

## 5. Data-mining bias: why ML makes overfitting easier, and the required rigor

The math is unforgiving. With N strategy configurations tried, the expected maximum in-sample Sharpe rises with √log N even when all true Sharpes are zero; the Deflated Sharpe Ratio corrects for exactly this selection bias, non-normality, and trial count ([Bailey & Lopez de Prado 2014, JPM, SSRN 2460551](https://papers.ssrn.com/sol3/papers.cfm?abstract_id=2460551)). Bailey, Borwein, Lopez de Prado & Zhu show that even short series of IID returns can be "fit" into impressive backtests, and derive the minimum backtest length needed for a claimed Sharpe ([Pseudo-Mathematics and Financial Charlatanism, Notices of the AMS](https://www.ams.org/notices/201405/rnoti-p458.pdf)); their Combinatorially-Symmetric CV yields the Probability of Backtest Overfitting ([publications list](https://www.quantresearch.org/Publications.htm)). Harvey, Liu & Zhu (2016, RFS) argue a new factor needs a t-stat above ~3.0, not 2.0, precisely because of multiple testing ([SSRN 2249314](https://papers.ssrn.com/sol3/papers.cfm?abstract_id=2249314)); Harvey & Liu (2015, JPM) give the corresponding Sharpe-ratio haircuts ([Duke PDF](https://people.duke.edu/~charvey/Research/Published_Papers/P120_Backtesting.PDF)). For trading rules specifically: Sullivan, Timmermann & White (1999, JF) showed the best technical rules' outperformance is consistent with data snooping once White's Reality Check is applied ([doi.org/10.1111/0022-1082.00163](https://doi.org/10.1111/0022-1082.00163); [White 2000, Econometrica](https://doi.org/10.1111/1468-0262.00152)), and Bajgrowicz & Scaillet (2012, JFE) showed the apparent performance of Dow Jones technical rules since 1897 disappears after false-discovery correction and costs ([JFE 106(3):473-491](https://ideas.repec.org/a/eee/jfinec/v106y2012i3p473-491.html)). ML multiplies the trial count by orders of magnitude — every architecture, feature set, threshold, and prompt variant is a "trial" — which is why Arnott, Harvey & Markowitz (2019) demand an explicit protocol: economic rationale first, disclosed trial counts, deflated metrics, and model-free baselines ([JFDS, SSRN 3275654](https://papers.ssrn.com/sol3/papers.cfm?abstract_id=3275654)). Lopez de Prado's [10 reasons most ML funds fail](https://doi.org/10.3905/jpm.2018.44.6.120) are process failures (siloed research, backtest overfitting, wrong labeling/weighting), not modeling failures.

Concrete rules that follow from this literature: (1) purged walk-forward or combinatorially-purged CV with an embargo ≥ the label horizon; (2) sample weights for label overlap; (3) report PBO and Deflated Sharpe, not raw Sharpe; (4) every parameter/threshold/prompt change increments the trial count N used in the deflation; (5) a frozen, untouched out-of-sample window plus a forward paper-trading period before any component goes live; (6) any ML layer must beat the simplest non-ML baseline net of costs, not just improve in-sample.

## 6. Realistic assessment for a £100k retail FX account

- Your market is the most efficient one studied. Nothing in the literature suggests a retail-scale GBM can predict EURUSD direction net of spread/commission; the credible FX edges are slow, simple, and capacity-insensitive: trend/momentum ([Hurst et al. 2017](https://doi.org/10.3905/jpm.2017.44.1.015); [Menkhoff et al. 2012](https://cepr.org/voxeu/columns/limits-currency-momentum-trading)) — with known multi-year flat spells and 30–50% cost drag.
- Costs dominate at this scale. Even for institutions, momentum costs consume a large share of gross returns ([Menkhoff VoxEU](https://cepr.org/voxeu/columns/limits-currency-momentum-trading)); equity momentum profits were shown to be largely illusory for average investors after costs ([Lesmond, Schill & Zhou 2004, JFE](https://ideas.repec.org/a/eee/jfinec/v71y2004i2p349-380.html)); AQR's $1.7tn of live trades show costs are lower *only* for sophisticated execution ([Frazzini, Israel & Moskowitz, "Trading Costs"](https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.3229719); [coverage](https://www.institutionalinvestor.com/article/2bsxq9yadiccew11t8idc/portfolio/aqr-investor-worries-over-trading-costs-may-be-overblown)). For a retail FX account this argues for *fewer, larger, longer-holding trades* — which also shrinks the sample available to train any ML layer, worsening the small-data problem AQR flags ([SSRN 3624052](https://ssrn.com/abstract=3624052)).
- On simplicity vs. optimization: naive equal-weight portfolios beat most optimized allocations out-of-sample because estimation error swamps the model ([DeMiguel, Garlappi & Uppal 2009, RFS](https://doi.org/10.1093/rfs/hhm075)). The same logic applies one level down: a robust rule plus fixed vol-target sizing beats a finely-tuned ML overlay at retail sample sizes.
- Credible quants on "minimum infrastructure before ML adds value": AQR's verdict is ML's reliable uses are risk modeling and cost modeling, with return prediction a small-data problem ([SSRN 3624052](https://ssrn.com/abstract=3624052)); Lopez de Prado's failure taxonomy is all process — implying that point-in-time data, purged CV, deflated metrics, and trial accounting are *prerequisites*, and without them ML output is indistinguishable from noise ([doi.org/10.3905/jpm.2018.44.6.120](https://doi.org/10.3905/jpm.2018.44.6.120)). This codebase already has point-in-time data and a CPCV harness (`meta_labeling.py:26-29`) — the infrastructure bar is closer to met than the *evidence* bar for each component.
- Where ML/LLM effort is defensible here: (i) risk/cost estimation (spread regime detection, slippage models); (ii) vol-targeted sizing and momentum crash avoidance (§4); (iii) LLMs as text classifiers converting news/Fed-speak into structured features ([Hansen & Kazinnik](https://www.frbsf.org/research-and-insights/publications/system-research-richmond-fed/2024/03/can-chatgpt-decipher-fedspeak/)); (iv) post-trade *journaling for a human* (not as a prompt input). Where it is not: direction prediction on majors, prompt-fed "lessons," and auto-mined rules promoted to production without the §5 gauntlet.

## 7. Verdicts

**(a) Rule-based momentum signal — KEEP, with CHANGES.** It is the only component with a century-scale, cross-asset, FX-inclusive evidence base ([Moskowitz et al. 2012](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.jfineco.2011.11.003); [Hurst et al. 2017](https://doi.org/10.3905/jpm.2017.44.1.015); [Menkhoff et al. 2012](https://ideas.repec.org/a/eee/jfinec/v106y2012i3p660-684.html); [Hsu et al. 2016](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0022199616300472)). Change: add fixed vol-targeted sizing and a strategy-vol spike stand-down rule (crash avoidance, [Barroso & Santa-Clara](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.jfineco.2014.11.010); [Daniel & Moskowitz](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.jfineco.2015.12.002)); cut turnover to control the 30–50% cost drag; pre-register decay expectations (~50% post-discovery, [McLean & Pontiff via Robeco](https://www.robeco.com/docm/docu-202003-how-to-navigate-the-factor-zoo-us.pdf)) and multi-year flat spells so the system isn't "self-healed" out of a real edge during a normal drawdown.

**(b) GBM meta-labeling — CHANGE (keep only as a gated, brutally-validated veto; drop if it can't beat the baseline).** No peer-reviewed live-edge evidence; practitioners find it mostly filters trade count ([QuantConnect](https://www.quantconnect.com/forum/discussion/14706/why-meta-labeling-is-not-a-silver-bullet/); [Quant.SE](https://quant.stackexchange.com/questions/50082/why-meta-labeling-is-is-robust)). Required before it stays live: purge+embargo spanning the full barrier horizon, uniqueness weights, PBO and Deflated Sharpe reported, the veto threshold counted as a trial, and a frozen forward A/B against primary-only trading net of costs. If the veto can't beat "primary-only" (and a trivial regime win-rate filter) out-of-sample, **DROP** it — at a few hundred FX trades it is the system's single most overfit-able component ([Harvey & Liu](https://people.duke.edu/~charvey/Research/Published_Papers/P120_Backtesting.PDF); [Bailey & Lopez de Prado](https://papers.ssrn.com/sol3/papers.cfm?abstract_id=2460551)).

**(c) LLM filters — SPLIT.**
- *News-sentiment veto:* **CHANGE.** Permissible only as a veto-only, fail-ALLOW risk overlay evaluated strictly on post-cutoff, logged-forward decisions (pre-cutoff backtests are unidentified due to memorization: [arXiv:2504.14765](https://arxiv.org/abs/2504.14765); [SSRN 4754678](https://papers.ssrn.com/sol3/papers.cfm?abstract_id=4754678)); anonymize instruments/entities in headlines where feasible ([arXiv:2309.17322](https://arxiv.org/abs/2309.17322)); pin and regression-test model versions because behavior drifts ([arXiv:2307.09009](https://arxiv.org/abs/2307.09009)). Evidence for durable *FX* edge: none published — treat as an unproven risk overlay, size its authority accordingly.
- *LLM "lessons" fed back into prompts:* **DROP as a trading input** (keep as human-facing journaling). No supporting evidence; hallucination and drift are documented properties ([arXiv:2202.03629](https://arxiv.org/abs/2202.03629); [arXiv:2307.09009](https://arxiv.org/abs/2307.09009)); and each lesson is an unvalidated, invisible strategy change that defeats the §5 trial accounting. Replace with deterministic, computed statistics (win rate by setup/regime) if the prompt needs context.
- *LLM-mined rules:* **DROP as a production input; KEEP as hypothesis generation only.** Every mined rule must enter the same pipeline as a human idea: economic rationale, purged OOS, deflated metrics with the full trial count ([Arnott, Harvey & Markowitz](https://papers.ssrn.com/sol3/papers.cfm?abstract_id=3275654)). Absent that, LLM mining is an industrial-scale data-snooping machine — the exact failure mode Sullivan-Timmermann-White and Bajgrowicz-Scaillet documented for rule mining pre-LLM ([STW](https://doi.org/10.1111/0022-1082.00163); [Bajgrowicz & Scaillet](https://ideas.repec.org/a/eee/jfinec/v106y2012i3p473-491.html)).

**Bottom line:** the evidence supports spending the "AI budget" on risk discipline (vol-targeted sizing, crash avoidance, cost/slippage modeling) and validation machinery — where ML-adjacent techniques have demonstrated value — rather than on predictive layers, where even well-resourced institutions find small, decaying, cost-sensitive edges in far richer data than a retail FX account can produce.
